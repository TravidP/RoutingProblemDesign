One-sentence takeaway

Intersections can be treated as non-monetary “tolls” by strategically shaping waiting time, so self-interested drivers are nudged toward socially efficient route choices without payments or major road changes.

The thesis reframes intersections from passive bottlenecks into active mechanism-design interfaces. Modern V2I/autonomous intersection management lets a roadside unit alter priority, service order, or effective request time. Those small local changes become path-dependent costs, which influence Wardrop equilibria at the network level.

Figure. Conceptual map of the thesis. The key loop is: decentralized route choice creates inefficient user equilibrium; autonomous intersections introduce controllable delay levers; priority or timestamp mechanisms create path-dependent node costs; a planner designs these incentives while anticipating the new equilibrium; the same framework suggests PhD extensions in dynamics, mixed autonomy, fairness, privacy, spillback, and scalable bilevel algorithms.

Problem setting

Why selfish routing is inefficient

Drivers choose routes that minimize their own perceived travel time. The resulting Wardrop equilibrium is stable because no used route is more expensive than an alternative, but it can differ from the system optimum that minimizes total travel time.

The gap is measured by the Price of Anarchy:

PoA = total cost at user equilibrium / total cost at system optimum

Pigou and Braess examples motivate why “more capacity” or naive routing can worsen network behavior when users react strategically.

Main thesis move

Why intersections are the right lever

Most congestion-game models emphasize edge costs, but urban delay often occurs at intersections. Intersections already impose waiting time, so the thesis asks whether that delay can be designed as an incentive rather than accepted as a nuisance.

Autonomous intersection management makes this plausible because vehicles can send identifiers, timestamps, intended movements, or route information to roadside units that schedule crossing times.

Core technical contribution

Two complementary intersection-based mechanisms

1) Strategic Priority-Based Scheduling (SPBS)

Intuition: give higher intersection priority to vehicles on socially desirable routes and lower priority to routes that are overused at equilibrium.

Analytical benchmark: Pigou’s two-route network. FCFS gives equal node delay, so the classical inefficient equilibrium persists. SPBS creates asymmetric node delays:

upper-route node delay = w - p lower-route node delay = w + p

Key result: for polynomial lower-link cost x_l^n, the SPBS equilibrium lower-route flow becomes:

x_l^e = (1 - 2p)^(1/n), for p in [0, 1/2) x_l^e = 0, for p >= 1/2

Interpretation: any positive priority asymmetry reduces the user-equilibrium cost relative to FCFS in the stylized model. SUMO simulations support the qualitative behavior.

2) Timestamp-based node-cost incentives

Intuition: instead of categorical priority classes, the planner advances or delays a vehicle’s effective request timestamp before the RSU schedules it.

Analytical model: each intersection contributes a baseline node delay plus a path-dependent timestamp offset:

c_v^p(f_v, u_v^p) = c_v(f_v) + u_v^p

The full path cost is additive across edges and nodes:

c_p(f,u) = Σ_{e in p} c_e(f_e) + Σ_{v in p} c_v^p(f_v,u_v^p)

Key result: under monotone, continuous costs, the induced routing game admits a potential-function characterization, equilibrium existence, and essential uniqueness in social cost.

Planner-vs-driver structure

The bilevel formulation in plain language

The planner does not directly assign routes. Instead, it chooses timestamp incentives. Drivers then selfishly react to the modified costs. This creates a Stackelberg-style bilevel problem.

Lower level: driver equilibrium

For fixed incentives, drivers settle into a Wardrop equilibrium. The thesis shows this can be computed by minimizing a modified potential:

f(u) ∈ argmin_f [ Φ⁰(f) + f · u ]

Upper level: system planner

The planner chooses implementable incentives to minimize the resulting total cost:

min_u C⁰(f(u)) + f(u) · u

Operational constraints include timestamp bounds, same-lane order preservation, minimum headways, and limits caused by incomplete path observability.

Strengths and weaknesses

What is solid, and what remains open

Strong points

  • Clear mechanism-design interpretation of intersection delays.
  • Bridges simple analytical examples, general congestion-game theory, and microscopic simulation.
  • Preserves tractability through additive path-dependent node costs and potential functions.
  • Offers a non-monetary alternative to tolls, credits, and road expansion.

Important limitations

  • Core theory is static and nonatomic; transient queues and discrete vehicle interactions are simplified.
  • Separable monotone node/link costs may fail under spillback, blocking, or lane coupling.
  • Deployment assumes sufficient V2I capability and safe timestamp manipulation.
  • Bilevel optimization is expensive: the reported setup uses many equilibrium solves.
  • Behavioral compliance, privacy, and fairness need deeper treatment.

A 90-day research plan from this thesis

Reproduce the baseline

Implement a small Pigou/SPBS case and a Sioux Falls static traffic assignment baseline. Verify UE, SO, and PoA calculations before adding incentives.

Build one clean extension

Choose one extension only: fairness constraints, dynamic demand, mixed autonomy, privacy, or spillback. Start with a model simple enough to prove something.

Create a simulation bridge

Use SUMO to calibrate or stress-test the extension. Keep a reproducible pipeline: network files, demand generation, calibration data, optimization logs, and metrics.

Compare against baselines

Benchmark against FCFS, classical system-optimal routing, marginal-cost tolling, and simple information-routing policies where appropriate.

Write a first paper around one theorem + one experiment

A strong first paper could pair a theoretical guarantee with a medium-scale numerical demonstration, rather than trying to solve every deployment issue at once.

Useful glossary
Wardrop equilibrium

A flow pattern where every used path has minimal perceived cost; no infinitesimal driver can improve by switching routes alone.

System optimum

The flow allocation that minimizes total travel time or social cost across all users.

Price of Anarchy

The ratio between selfish-equilibrium cost and system-optimal cost. Larger values indicate greater inefficiency from decentralization.

Path-dependent node cost

An intersection delay term that depends on both node congestion and the path chosen by a vehicle, enabling intersections to influence route choices.

Bilevel optimization

An optimization problem where the upper-level planner selects incentives while the lower level represents drivers’ equilibrium response.
